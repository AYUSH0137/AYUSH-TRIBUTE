A.P.J ABDUL KALAM

Former Indian president and Indian scientist

"Don’t read success stories, you will only get a message. Read failure stories, you will get some ideas to get success."

Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam was born in 15 October 1931 – 27 July 2015 was an Indian aerospace scientist and statesman who served as the 11th president of India from 2002 to 2007. Born and raised in a Muslim family in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, he studied physics and aerospace engineering. He spent the next four decades as a scientist and science administrator, mainly at the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) and Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) and was intimately involved in India's civilian space programme and military missile development efforts. He thus came to be known as the Missile Man of India for his work on the development of ballistic missile and launch vehicle technology. He also played a pivotal organisational, technical, and political role in India's Pokhran-II nuclear tests in 1998, the first since the original nuclear test by India in 1974.

Kalam was elected as the 11th president of India in 2002 with the support of both the ruling Bharatiya Janata Party and the then-opposition Indian National Congress. Widely referred to as the "People's President", he returned to his civilian life of education, writing and public service after a single term. He was a recipient of several prestigious awards, including the Bharat Ratna, India's highest civilian honour.

While delivering a lecture at the Indian Institute of Management Shillong, Kalam collapsed and died from an apparent cardiac arrest on 27 July 2015, aged 83. Thousands, including national-level dignitaries, attended the funeral ceremony held in his hometown of Rameswaram, where he was buried with full state honours.

Career Achievements

  • Early in his career at DRDO, he helped build India’s first indigenous hovercraft, named Nandi.
  • He was part of the team that set up TERLS, which is still used by ISRO for launching sounding rockets.
  • Supervised the successful launch of India’s first Satellite Launch Vehicle.
  • Led the IGMDP, which resulted in the development of key missiles like Agni and Prithvi.
  • Served as the Chief Scientific Adviser to the Prime Minister and Secretary of DRDO from July 1992 to December 1991.
  • Played a pivotal role in the successful execution of the Pokhran-II nuclear tests in 1998.
  • Served as the 11th President of India from 2002 to 2007.
  • Authored several influential books, including “Wings of Fire” and “India 2020,” which outline his vision for transforming India into a developed nation.
  • Received India’s highest civilian honor, the Bharat Ratna, for his contributions to scientific research and modernization of defense technology.

Legacy

Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam’s legacy is vast and multifaceted, touching upon various aspects of Indian society, science, and inspirational leadership. Here are some key elements of his legacy:
Scientific Innovation: Known as the “Missile Man of India,” Dr. Kalam’s work in developing ballistic missile and launch vehicle technology is a cornerstone of his legacy.
Presidential Tenure: As the 11th President of India, he was widely respected for his humility, wisdom, and accessibility, making him a beloved leader.
Educational Influence: His enduring commitment to education and progress, particularly his efforts to inspire young minds, remains a significant part of his legacy. Vision for India: Dr. Kalam’s vision for India, outlined in his books and speeches, aimed at transforming India into a developed nation by 2020.
Role Model: His life is an embodiment of perseverance, knowledge, and determination, serving as a role model for millions. Cultural Impact: Dr. Kalam’s thoughts and teachings continue to influence various spheres of life, including science, education, and leadership. Dr. Kalam’s legacy continues to inspire and motivate people across India and the world, encouraging them to dream big and work hard to realize those dreams.
